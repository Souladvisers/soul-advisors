Foundation

Why Islamic Finance?

Singapore's Muslim community represents over 15% of the population. Yet most financial advisors — including Muslim ones — lack the specific knowledge to address Islamic financial concerns with confidence. This gap is your opportunity.

Many Muslim clients hold back from financial planning not because they don't want to plan, but because they are uncertain whether the products being recommended are Shariah-compliant. They may have unresolved questions about interest (riba), unclear investment funds, or unaddressed estate planning concerns like Faraid. An FC who can speak to these concerns knowledgeably — without pretending to be an Islamic scholar — builds a level of trust that is very hard for other advisors to match.

"Prepare for them whatever you are able of strength..." (Al-Anfal 8:60)

Foundations

Core Shariah Principles

Shariah-compliant finance is built on a set of foundational principles derived from the Quran and Sunnah. Understanding these helps you explain why certain products are acceptable and others are not.

🚫

Riba — Interest / Usury

Any predetermined fixed return on money is considered riba and is prohibited. This includes conventional bank interest, bond coupons, and any guaranteed return on a loan. Riba is one of the most serious prohibitions in Islamic finance.

FC application: Conventional savings accounts, fixed deposits, and interest-bearing bonds are problematic. Islamic alternatives use profit-sharing (Mudarabah) or cost-plus (Murabaha) structures instead.

🎲

Gharar — Excessive Uncertainty

Contracts with excessive ambiguity or uncertainty in key terms are prohibited. This is why conventional insurance is considered problematic by some scholars — the premium is paid but it is uncertain whether any benefit will be received.

FC application: Takaful resolves this through a mutual contribution model rather than a commercial exchange contract.

🎰

Maisir — Gambling / Speculation

Transactions that resemble gambling — where gain is entirely dependent on chance with no underlying economic activity — are prohibited. This affects certain derivatives and highly speculative instruments.

FC application: Explains why pure speculation in leveraged instruments is not Shariah-compliant, even if the underlying asset is halal.

🤝

What IS Permitted

Trade, profit-sharing, equity ownership in halal businesses, and risk-sharing arrangements are all permitted. The key distinction: money must be earned through real economic activity, not simply by virtue of having money.

FC application: Equity investment in screened companies, Sukuk (Islamic bonds), Mudarabah savings, and Takaful are the building blocks of a Shariah-compliant portfolio.

Additional Prohibited Activities

Beyond the core prohibitions, investments must avoid sectors that are fundamentally impermissible (haram):

✅ Halal Sectors

  • Technology (screened)
  • Healthcare & pharmaceuticals
  • Consumer goods (halal)
  • Real estate (excluding REITs with haram tenants)
  • Utilities
  • Manufacturing (screened)
  • Infrastructure
  • Renewable energy

✗ Haram Sectors

  • Conventional banking & insurance
  • Alcohol production & distribution
  • Pork & non-halal food
  • Tobacco
  • Gambling & casinos
  • Weapons & defence (controversial)
  • Adult entertainment
  • Interest-based financial services

Key Terms Glossary

These are the terms you will encounter in Islamic finance conversations. Knowing them helps you speak with Muslim clients at a level that earns their respect and trust.

Shariah(شريعة)
Islamic law derived from the Quran and Hadith. In finance, Shariah principles govern what is permissible (halal) and prohibited (haram) in transactions and investments.
Riba(ربا)
Interest or usury. Any predetermined, fixed return on money lent is considered riba. This is one of the most serious prohibitions in Islamic law.
Halal(حلال)
Permissible. In finance: instruments and activities allowed under Shariah.
Haram(حرام)
Prohibited. Activities, sectors, or instruments that violate Shariah principles.
Takaful(تكافل)
Shariah-compliant insurance. Based on mutual contribution and shared risk among participants, rather than a commercial exchange contract. Structured to avoid riba, gharar, and maisir.
Mudarabah(مضاربة)
Profit-sharing arrangement. One party provides capital, the other provides expertise and management. Profits are shared according to a pre-agreed ratio; losses fall on the capital provider.
Sukuk(صكوك)
Islamic bonds. Unlike conventional bonds, Sukuk represent ownership in a tangible asset and pay returns from the asset's income — not from interest. Often called "Islamic bonds" but structurally different.
Zakat(زكاة)
Obligatory almsgiving — the third pillar of Islam. 2.5% of wealth held above the nisab (minimum threshold) for one lunar year. Purifies wealth and redistributes it to eligible recipients.
Nisab(نصاب)
The minimum threshold of wealth that must be held for one full lunar year before Zakat becomes obligatory. Pegged to the value of 85 grams of gold or 595 grams of silver.
Faraid(فرائض)
Islamic inheritance law. Prescribes fixed shares of an estate for specific heirs. In Singapore, applied to assets subject to Muslim inheritance law — but NOT CPF or life insurance proceeds.
Hibah(هبة)
Gift. A voluntary, immediate transfer of ownership during the donor's lifetime. Can be used as a wealth-transfer tool that bypasses Faraid, as it transfers ownership before death.
Wasiat(وصية)
Islamic will. Limited to a maximum of one-third of the estate and can only be given to non-heirs (since heirs already receive their Faraid shares). Cannot override Faraid.
Waqf(وقف)
Islamic endowment. An irrevocable charitable trust where assets are dedicated in perpetuity for the benefit of a charitable cause. One of the most powerful Islamic wealth-preservation and legacy tools.
Purification (Tazkiyah)(تزكية)
The process of removing impure returns (e.g., from a small percentage of haram income in a screened fund) by donating that proportion to charity — without expecting reward.
Shariah-Compliant Investing

Shariah Screening

Not all equity funds are Shariah-compliant — but Shariah-compliant funds are not simply "Muslim versions" of conventional funds. They are subject to ongoing screening by an independent Shariah Supervisory Board (SSB).

How Shariah Screening Works

1

Sector Screen (Qualitative)

The fund excludes companies whose primary business is in haram sectors: alcohol, pork, tobacco, conventional banking, gambling, adult entertainment, and weapons (varies by standard). A company that derives even a significant minority of revenue from these sectors may be excluded.

2

Financial Ratios Screen (Quantitative)

Even halal-sector companies must pass financial ratio tests to ensure they are not excessively leveraged with interest-bearing debt. Common thresholds: total interest-bearing debt must be less than 33% of total assets, and interest income must be less than 5% of total revenue.

3

Ongoing Monitoring

Screening is not a one-time event. The Shariah Supervisory Board reviews holdings regularly. If a company fails the screen (e.g., after an acquisition or change in business), it is removed from the fund. Purification amounts are calculated and disclosed.

4

Annual Purification Calculation

Even in a screened fund, some income may be derived from minor impermissible sources (e.g., a company earns 2% from interest). The fund calculates the purification amount per unit and advises investors to donate that amount to charity.

Shariah-Compliant Investing

Purification of Returns

Even in a Shariah-screened fund, a small portion of corporate revenue may come from impermissible sources. Purification is the process of donating that proportion to charity — removing the "taint" from your investment returns.

How Purification Works in Practice

Example

Your client has $50,000 invested in a Shariah-compliant global equity fund. The fund's SSB calculates the annual purification rate as 0.4% of the invested amount (based on the proportion of impermissible income across all holdings).

Purification amount = $50,000 × 0.4% = $200

The client should donate $200 to charity (not as zakat — purification has no reward, it is simply disposal of impure income). The donation can go to any legitimate charitable cause.

💡 Key Point: Purification ≠ Zakat

Many clients confuse purification with Zakat. They are distinct obligations. Zakat is a reward-bearing act of worship — giving from your halal wealth to specific recipients. Purification is disposal of impure income — no spiritual reward is earned from it. However, the act of purifying wealth itself is meritorious in intention.

The purification amount is usually very small — often less than 1% of invested value per year. Most fund managers provide a purification calculator or state the rate in the annual report. Advising your client about purification shows depth of knowledge and earns significant trust.

Takaful vs Conventional Insurance

Conventional insurance is viewed by many Islamic scholars as containing elements of gharar (uncertainty), riba (if there are guaranteed interest components), and maisir (gambling-like structure). Takaful was developed as the Shariah-compliant alternative.

The Structural Difference

Conventional Insurance

Structure: Policyholder pays premium to insurer in exchange for a promise of compensation. It is a commercial sale contract (exchange of premium for risk transfer).

Shariah concern: The element of uncertainty (you pay but may never claim = gharar). If there is a guaranteed return component with interest = riba. Profit belongs entirely to the insurer.

Takaful

Structure: Participants contribute to a mutual fund (tabarru' — donation). Claims are paid from this fund. A Takaful operator manages the fund for a fee. Surplus belongs to participants, not the company.

Shariah resolution: Based on mutual solidarity (ta'awun) and donation (tabarru'). No riba, gharar minimised by transparency, no speculative element.

Zakat

What is Zakat?

Zakat is the third pillar of Islam — an obligatory annual contribution of 2.5% of qualifying wealth above the nisab threshold, held for a full lunar year. It is not charity in the conventional sense — it is a right of the poor over the wealth of the rich.

خُذْ مِنْ أَمْوَالِهِمْ صَدَقَةً تُطَهِّرُهُمْ وَتُزَكِّيهِم بِهَا

"Take from their wealth a charity by which you purify them and cause them increase..." (At-Tawbah 9:103)

Quran — on the nature and purpose of Zakat

The Key Conditions for Zakat Obligation

☪️

Muslim

Zakat is obligatory only for Muslims. Non-Muslim clients are not subject to Zakat.

💰

Above Nisab

Wealth must exceed the nisab (≈ value of 85g gold or 595g silver). Currently approximately SGD 7,000–8,000 (check MUIS annually).

📅

Full Hawl (Lunar Year)

The qualifying wealth must have been held above nisab for a complete Islamic lunar year (hawl) — not just on assessment date.

The 8 Recipients of Zakat

Zakat can only be given to eight categories of recipients, as specified in Surah At-Tawbah 9:60:

1. 🤲 The poor (Fuqara)
2. 🏚️ The needy (Masakin)
3. 💼 Zakat administrators (Amil)
4. ❤️ Those whose hearts are to be reconciled (Muallaf)
5. ⛓️ Those in bondage (Riqab)
6. 💳 The indebted (Gharimin)
7. ☪️ In the cause of Allah (Fi Sabilillah)
8. ✈️ Travellers in need (Ibn Sabil)
💡In Singapore, MUIS (Islamic Religious Council of Singapore) manages Zakat collection and distribution. Clients can pay Zakat through MUIS directly, or to approved Zakat collection agents. The MUIS Zakat calculator online is the simplest reference for Singapore-specific calculations.
Zakat

Zakat on Investments

Many Muslim investors are uncertain whether their investments are subject to Zakat — and if so, how much. This uncertainty causes some to either avoid investing or avoid Zakat altogether. You can help resolve this.

General Principle

The market value of invested assets held for trade or growth purposes is subject to Zakat. The rate is 2.5% of the total market value (not profit, not capital gain — the full value) if it has been held above nisab for a full lunar year.

Example Calculation

Client has $80,000 in a Shariah-compliant equity fund, $20,000 in a unit trust (non-Shariah), and $15,000 in a CPF Investment Account. All held for over a year. Nisab = $7,500.

Total zakatable investments = $80,000 + $20,000 + $15,000 = $115,000

Zakat due = $115,000 × 2.5% = $2,875

Note: Scholarly opinions vary on whether non-Shariah investments are zakatable — some say yes (the underlying wealth is still the client's), some say Zakat is not owed on haram wealth. Advise client to consult an Ustaz for certainty.

What About ILPs?

For Investment-Linked Plans, Zakat is assessed on the surrender value or investment account value at the time of assessment — not the total premiums paid. The protection component (cost of insurance) is not zakatable.

ILP Zakat — Practical Approach

1. Check the current investment account value (shown on annual policy statement)

2. Apply 2.5% if held above nisab for a full lunar year

3. For ILPs with a savings component, the surrender value is the zakatable amount

4. Include this in the client's overall Zakat calculation alongside other assets

Zakat on Insurance Proceeds

A common question: if a Muslim client receives a life insurance payout or a critical illness claim — is Zakat due on those proceeds?

The General Ruling

Insurance claim proceeds (death benefit, CI payout, disability income) are generally not subject to immediate Zakat at the point of receipt, as they are received in compensation for a loss or need, not as accumulated wealth. However, once received and held above the nisab for a full hawl (lunar year), the remaining amount becomes part of the client's general zakatable wealth.

Practical Example

A client receives a $200,000 life insurance payout after a bereavement. She immediately uses $150,000 to clear a mortgage. The remaining $50,000 is placed in a savings account. If that $50,000 remains above nisab after a full lunar year, Zakat is due on it as part of her regular annual Zakat calculation.

The $150,000 used to clear the mortgage is not zakatable — it has been expended on a liability.

💡Advising Muslim clients about this proactively — especially at policy inception — shows you understand their full picture. Many clients appreciate knowing that claiming on their policy does not trigger an unexpected Zakat obligation at the moment of their greatest need.

Zakat Calculation Guide

Use this simplified framework to help Muslim clients estimate their annual Zakat obligation — or to help them understand what to assess when doing so with MUIS.

Simplified Zakat on Savings & Investments (Annual)

Zakat-able Wealth =
  Cash & savings above living expenses
+ Market value of investment funds / unit trusts
+ ILP investment account value
+ Current value of endowments (surrender value)
+ Gold & silver (market value)
+ Business inventory (if applicable)
– Outstanding debts (that are currently due)

If total ≥ Nisab (~SGD 7,500 in 2025) AND held for ≥ 1 lunar year:
Zakat = Total × 2.5%

Not Included in Zakat Calculation

  • 🏠 Primary residence (the home you live in)
  • 🚗 Personal vehicle for own use
  • 👔 Personal clothing and household items
  • 💼 Business equipment and tools of trade
  • 📖 Professional tools (laptop, instruments used for earning income)
  • 🏥 Pure protection insurance premiums paid (consumed — not wealth)

✅ Refer Clients to MUIS for Official Calculation

The official MUIS Zakat calculator at muis.gov.sg covers specific categories in detail and reflects current nisab values pegged to Singapore gold prices. Your role is to make Muslim clients aware that their investments are likely zakatable and to help them gather the relevant figures — MUIS handles the definitive calculation.

Islamic Estate Planning

Faraid — Islamic Inheritance Law

Faraid is the Quranic system of inheritance that prescribes specific shares for specific heirs. In Singapore, it applies to the estate of a Muslim (assets owned in their sole name) — but does NOT automatically apply to CPF monies, joint assets, or life insurance proceeds. This distinction is crucial and widely misunderstood.

⚠️ The Most Common Misconception

Many Muslim clients believe that as long as they have a will (Wasiat), their estate will be distributed according to their wishes. This is only partly true. Faraid governs the distribution of the Islamic estate — a Wasiat can only direct up to 1/3 of the estate, and only to non-heirs. The remaining 2/3 must be distributed according to Faraid shares. An Islamic will does not override Faraid.

🚨 The CPF + Insurance "Faraid Gap"

Here is the critical insight for Muslim clients: because CPF and life insurance do NOT pass through the estate, they are not distributed according to Faraid. This means a Muslim client who wants to comply with Faraid for ALL their wealth must make deliberate nominations that mirror Faraid shares — or accept that CPF and insurance will be distributed differently. Many are unaware of this. Raising it demonstrates genuine expertise.

Islamic Estate Planning

Hibah

Hibah (gift) is one of the most powerful and underutilised tools in Islamic estate planning. It allows a Muslim to transfer wealth during their lifetime to whoever they choose — bypassing Faraid entirely — because the transfer occurs before death.

Key Features of Hibah

🎁

Lifetime Transfer

Hibah is completed during the donor's life. Ownership passes immediately upon acceptance — it is not a testamentary gift (it doesn't take effect at death).

🔓

Bypasses Faraid

Because the asset has transferred before death, it is no longer part of the estate — and therefore not subject to Faraid. A Muslim can give any asset to any person via Hibah.

⚠️

Irrevocable (Generally)

Once given and accepted, Hibah is generally irrevocable — particularly between non-family members. Parents can revoke Hibah to children in some madhabs. Get legal documentation.

Hibah in Insurance Context

The most common use of Hibah in financial planning is through Hibah Ruqba or simply structured insurance nominations that function as conditional gifts. A Muslim policy owner can nominate their policy proceeds as Hibah to a specific person — who will receive the full amount outside the estate, outside Faraid.

When Hibah is Useful

  • Client wants to leave more to a specific child (e.g., disabled child needing care) beyond their Faraid share
  • Client wants to provide for a non-Muslim spouse who would not inherit under Faraid
  • Client wants to gift assets to a grandchild whose parent (the heir) has predeceased them — grandchildren do not inherit under Faraid if the parent is deceased
  • Client wants to transfer business ownership to a specific child immediately
⚠️Hibah must be properly documented and the transfer must be genuine — an asset cannot be "gifted" on paper while the donor retains full control and use. Work with an Islamic estate lawyer to structure it correctly. Do not advise clients to use Hibah to circumvent legitimate Faraid claims from other heirs.
Islamic Estate Planning

Wasiat — The Islamic Will

A Wasiat is the Islamic will — a statement of how a Muslim wishes a portion of their estate to be distributed. It is an important tool, but it works within significant constraints that every FC should understand.

The 1/3 Rule

⚠️ Critical Limitation

A Wasiat can only cover up to 1/3 of the net estate (after debts and funeral expenses). The remaining 2/3 must be distributed according to Faraid.

Additionally, a Wasiat cannot be made to an heir who already receives a Faraid share — unless all other heirs consent. This means a Muslim cannot use a Wasiat to give extra to a son, daughter, or spouse beyond their Faraid entitlement (without the other heirs agreeing).

What Can a Wasiat Do?

✅ Permitted Uses

  • Give up to 1/3 to charity (waqf, mosque, etc.)
  • Give up to 1/3 to a non-Muslim spouse who wouldn't otherwise inherit
  • Give up to 1/3 to grandchildren whose parent has predeceased
  • Give up to 1/3 to a non-heir family member or friend
  • Direct funeral and burial arrangements
  • Appoint a guardian for minor children

❌ Not Permitted

  • Give more than 1/3 of estate to non-heirs
  • Give any Wasiat share to a Faraid heir (without all heirs consenting)
  • Override or change Faraid distribution
  • Use Wasiat to disinherit a legitimate heir
  • Make Wasiat conditional on surviving the testator (that's Faraid, not Wasiat)

In Singapore, Wasiat is executed through MUIS or through a Muslim-law-qualified lawyer. MUIS provides Wasiat writing services. Encourage clients to formalise their Wasiat even if they also have a civil will — the two instruments govern different aspects of the estate.

Islamic Estate Planning

Waqf — Islamic Endowment

Waqf is the most powerful and least discussed tool in Islamic estate planning. An irrevocable charitable endowment, Waqf removes assets from the estate permanently, preserves them across generations, and generates ongoing reward (sadaqah jariyah) for the donor.

How Waqf Works

1

Asset is Dedicated

The donor irrevocably dedicates an asset — cash, property, business income — for a charitable purpose. The asset leaves their personal ownership permanently.

2

Asset is Preserved

The Waqf asset cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited. It is maintained in perpetuity (or for a specified period) for the declared purpose.

3

Benefits are Distributed

The income or benefit from the Waqf asset is distributed to the specified beneficiaries — which can include the donor's own family (private Waqf) or the community (public Waqf).

4

Reward Continues

The Prophet ﷺ described sadaqah jariyah (ongoing charity) as one of three actions whose reward continues after death. A Waqf that continues to benefit others generates ongoing reward for the donor.

Types of Waqf

  • Private Waqf (Ahli): Benefits the donor's own family first, with any surplus going to a charitable cause.
  • Public Waqf (Khayri): Dedicated entirely to a public good — mosque, school, hospital, community service.
  • Cash Waqf: A monetary contribution managed by a Waqf administrator who invests it and distributes returns.

In Singapore, MUIS manages public Waqf. Private Waqf can be structured through specialised legal arrangements. Cash Waqf options are available through MUIS in small minimum amounts — accessible to middle-income clients, not just the wealthy.

Islamic Estate Planning

CPF & Islamic Law in Singapore

The intersection of CPF and Islamic inheritance law is one of the most misunderstood areas in Singapore's Muslim financial landscape. Getting this right is one of the most valuable conversations you can have with a Muslim client.

The Core Principle: CPF is Civil Law

CPF monies are governed by the CPF Act — Singapore civil law. They do NOT form part of the deceased's estate and are therefore NOT subject to Faraid. This is true regardless of the nominee's religion.

🚨 The "Forgotten Nomination" Problem

Scenario: A Muslim man made a CPF nomination to his first wife 20 years ago. He has since divorced and remarried. He has never updated his CPF nomination. When he dies, his CPF goes to his first wife — not his current family. This is legally valid under CPF Act. His current wife and children have no claim to it.

This scenario is tragically common. Every Muslim client should be asked: "When did you last check your CPF nomination?"

CPF Nomination Options for Muslim Clients

OptionWhat HappensFaraid Compliant?Recommended For
Nominate in Faraid proportions Nominee(s) receive shares matching their Faraid entitlement Yes Clients who want full Shariah compliance across all assets
Nominate spouse only Entire CPF goes to spouse — bypasses children's Faraid share No Client's personal choice — but should be made knowingly
No nomination CPF goes to Public Trustee, distributed under CPF Act (not Faraid) No Should be avoided — lack of planning, not a choice
Nominate to trust CPF paid to trustee who distributes per trust terms Depends on trust Clients with complex family needs or minor children

✅ The Action: Prompt Every Muslim Client to Review CPF Nominations

This is one of the simplest, highest-value actions you can take for a Muslim client. It costs nothing, takes 15 minutes on the CPF website, and protects their family. "When did you last review your CPF nomination?" should be a standard question in every annual review for Muslim clients.

Client Conversations

How to Have the Islamic Finance Conversation

The biggest mistake most FCs make with Muslim clients is either avoiding Islamic finance topics entirely (for fear of getting it wrong) or overclaiming expertise they don't have. The right approach is somewhere in the middle: informed, humble, and genuinely helpful.

Who Should You Raise This With?

Not every Muslim client wants an Islamic finance conversation. Some are happy with conventional products. Others are deeply observant and this is their primary filter. Read the room:

Signals that Islamic Finance is Important to Them

  • They ask directly: "Is this halal?"
  • They mention preference for Islamic banking / avoiding interest
  • They have opted for Takaful in the past
  • They reference Islamic obligations in financial decisions
  • They have waqf, hibah, or Faraid concerns about their estate
  • They express discomfort with investment products without knowing why

Opening the Conversation

Gentle Opener (for clients who haven't raised it)

"One thing I always like to check with Muslim clients — are Shariah-compliant options something that's important to you when it comes to your investments? Some clients prefer to keep within halal-screened funds, others are comfortable with the full range. There's no right or wrong answer — I just want to make sure we're aligned."

For estate planning discussions

"Given that you're Muslim, there's an important layer to your estate planning that goes beyond a standard will — specifically around Faraid, your CPF nominations, and how your insurance proceeds are directed. Have you had a chance to look at any of this? I'd like to make sure your estate is set up in a way that reflects both your financial goals and your values."

What to Say When You're Not Sure

The Honest Expert Response

"That's a great question — the honest answer is that scholarly opinions on this can vary, and I wouldn't want to give you a definitive ruling on what is or isn't halal. What I can do is help you understand the financial structure of this product and the options available to you, and I'd recommend you also consult with an Ustaz for the religious dimension. Together we can make sure you're comfortable from both angles."

💡Knowing when to refer is a sign of expertise, not weakness. Suggesting your client consult MUIS or an Islamic estate lawyer shows you understand the limits of your role — and it builds more trust than pretending to have all the answers.
Client Conversations

Common Scenarios

These are the conversations you are most likely to encounter with Muslim clients. Practise your responses to each until they feel natural.

Scenario 1: "I don't believe in insurance because of riba"

Context: A 32-year-old Muslim client, married with young children, has zero insurance coverage because he believes all insurance involves riba.

Response approach: Acknowledge his concern respectfully. Explain the distinction between conventional insurance and Takaful. Note that many scholars permit conventional insurance under darura (necessity) for coverage where Takaful is unavailable. Present Takaful options specifically. Do not dismiss his concern — it is theologically grounded — but help him see that a Shariah-compliant path exists.

Key phrase: "I completely understand your concern — and actually, there's a Shariah-compliant alternative called Takaful that was developed specifically to address this. Let me show you how it works."

Scenario 2: "I want my children to share equally — boys and girls the same"

Context: A Muslim client wants to divide assets equally among sons and daughters, contrary to Faraid (which gives sons double the daughters' share).

Response approach: This is a deeply personal and religious matter. Do not pass judgment. Explain what Faraid requires for assets in the estate. Explain that CPF and insurance nominations are outside Faraid and can be directed equally. Hibah (during lifetime) can be used to give equal gifts. Suggest they discuss with an Ustaz who can explain the theological position and whether any concessions apply in their madhab.

Key phrase: "For the assets in your estate, Faraid determines the shares. But for your CPF and insurance nominations — you have full flexibility to direct them however you choose. And if you want to equalise during your lifetime, Hibah is an option. Would you like me to walk through how these different tools work together?"

Scenario 3: "My non-Muslim wife can't inherit from me — what can I do?"

Context: A Muslim man married to a non-Muslim woman is concerned she will be left with nothing if he dies, since non-Muslims cannot inherit under Faraid.

Response approach: This is a genuine and important concern. Tools available: (1) Life insurance nomination to wife — directly bypasses estate/Faraid; (2) CPF nomination to wife — also bypasses estate; (3) Hibah — gift assets during lifetime; (4) Wasiat up to 1/3 of estate (some scholars permit this to a non-Muslim spouse; others don't — Ustaz consultation needed). The key insight: careful financial planning can ensure the wife is well provided for despite the Faraid constraint.

Key phrase: "You're right that Faraid doesn't cover non-Muslim spouses. But here's the good news — your CPF nomination and life insurance nomination can go directly to your wife, completely outside the estate and Faraid. We can structure your financial plan so that she is well taken care of."

Final Knowledge Check

A Muslim client has a $500,000 life insurance policy with no nomination. He passes away, leaving a wife and two sons. Which of the following is true?
The $500,000 is distributed according to Faraid — wife gets 1/8, sons share the rest.
The $500,000 forms part of the estate and is distributed via Muslim inheritance law.
Because there is no nomination, the $500,000 is paid to the "proper claimant" under the Insurance Act (up to $150,000 without Grant of Probate; above that requires legal process). The distribution is governed by civil law, not Faraid — though the court may apply Muslim law if the estate goes through probate.
✓ Correct. Life insurance without a nomination is governed by civil law, not automatically by Faraid. Without a nomination, the process is more complex, slower, and uncertain. This is exactly why every client — Muslim or not — must have current, valid insurance nominations. For Muslim clients, this is doubly important because of the Faraid implications.
✗ This is the most common misconception. Life insurance does NOT automatically pass through the estate or Faraid — it requires a valid nomination to be paid directly to beneficiaries. Without one, the process is governed by the Insurance Act and civil law, not Muslim inheritance law.
