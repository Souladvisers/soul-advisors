Foundation

What is Financial Planning?

Financial planning is not about selling products. It is the process of helping a client understand where they are today, where they want to be, and building a structured roadmap to get there — using the right financial tools at the right time.

Most FCs start their career selling policies. The best FCs transition to being financial planners who happen to use insurance and investment products as tools. This shift changes everything — how you prospect, how you fact-find, how you present, and how clients see you.

"A doctor doesn't prescribe medicine before running tests. A financial planner doesn't recommend products before understanding the client's full picture."

SOUL Guild Training Philosophy

The 3 Questions Every Client Has

Whether they say it out loud or not, every client who sits in front of you is asking three questions:

🤔

Am I OK?

What would happen if I got sick, became disabled, or died tomorrow? Can my family survive?

📅

Will I be OK?

Will I have enough to retire comfortably? Can I fund my children's education?

🏛️

What happens after?

What happens to my assets and dependants if something happens to me? Is my legacy protected?

Financial planning, done well, answers all three. Your job is to guide them to those answers — not to sell them something and leave.

Framework

The Planning Wheel

Financial planning covers six interconnected areas. Think of them as a wheel — if one section is flat, the whole ride is bumpy. Your job is to help clients build a complete, balanced wheel.

The 6 Areas of Financial Planning

🧠 How to Use the Wheel

In your first client meeting, briefly walk through all 6 areas. Ask "How comfortable are you with this area?" or "Have you thought about this?" You are not selling yet — you are helping them see their own gaps. Most clients will identify 2–3 areas they have never thought about. That's your long-term pipeline.

Where to Start?

Always start where the client is most anxious. If a young father walks in worried about what happens to his family if he dies — start with Protection. If a 45-year-old is panicking about retirement — start there. The wheel has no mandatory entry point. Meeting the client where they are builds trust faster than any script.

💡After covering the client's priority area, say: "Now that we've addressed your main concern, can I quickly share 2 other areas that many people in your situation tend to overlook?" This naturally opens up the rest of the wheel.

Pillar 1

Protection

Protection is the foundation. Without it, everything else you build can be wiped out by a single event — a death, a critical illness, a disability. Before investment, before retirement, before anything — the foundation must be solid.

💀
Type 1
Death / TPD
Replaces income and clears liabilities if the client dies or is permanently disabled.
🩺
Type 2
Critical Illness
Lump sum on diagnosis of a covered illness — used to fund treatment, replace income, clear debt.
🏥
Type 3
Hospitalisation
Covers inpatient and surgical bills. PRUShield in Singapore context — integrated with MediShield Life.
♿
Type 4
Disability Income
Monthly income replacement if the client is unable to work due to illness or injury.
🧓
Type 5
Long-Term Care
CareShield Life supplements. Covers daily benefits when client cannot perform basic ADLs.
🏠
Type 6
Mortgage Protection
Ensures the home loan is covered if the primary earner passes away. Often a decreasing term plan.

The Human Life Value Concept

The easiest way to show a client how much they need is to help them see their own economic value — not as a person, but as an income-generating machine.

Human Life Value Formula
Annual Income × Years to Retirement = Basic Life Coverage Needed

Example: $5,000/month × 12 × 30 years = $1,800,000
Less: existing coverage = Coverage Gap

But income replacement is just the floor. You also need to add: outstanding mortgage balance, children's education fund, spouse's retirement top-up, and 1–2 years of living expenses as a buffer. Most Singaporeans are underinsured by 30–50% on death coverage and often have zero disability or CI coverage.

Protection Conversation Framework

1

Make It Personal

"If you couldn't work tomorrow — who would be affected? Your spouse? Your parents? How long would your savings last?" Make them feel the gap before you fill it.

2

Quantify the Gap

Use the HLV formula on the spot. Show them a number. "Based on what you've shared, your family would need approximately $X to maintain their lifestyle. You currently have $Y covered. The gap is $Z."

3

Priority Stack

If budget is tight, help them prioritise: hospitalisation first (medical costs are unpredictable), then CI (most impactful during working years), then death (dependants), then disability.

4

Term vs Whole Life Conversation

Explain simply: term is renting coverage, whole life is owning it. Most clients need a mix — term for large short-term needs (mortgage, young children), whole life or CI for permanent needs. There is no universally correct answer.

⚠️MAS Suitability: Any recommendation must be based on the client's documented financial situation, needs, and risk appetite. Never recommend a product you haven't fact-found for. Keep your FNA records updated.

Knowledge Check

A 35-year-old client earns $6,000/month, has a $600,000 mortgage, two young children, and existing death coverage of $300,000. Which of the following most accurately describes their situation?
They are adequately covered — $300,000 is a solid amount of insurance.
They have a significant protection gap — their HLV alone is ~$1.8M, plus the $600K mortgage, so they need roughly $2M+ in coverage and currently have only $300K.
They should focus on investment first before buying more insurance.
✓ Correct. HLV = $6,000 × 12 × 30 years = $2.16M. Plus $600K mortgage = $2.76M needed. Existing: $300K. Gap: ~$2.46M. This is a very common situation for young Singapore families.
✗ Review the HLV formula. A $300K death policy on a $6K/month income with a $600K mortgage and two children is severely underinsured.
Pillar 2

Savings & Emergency Fund

Before your client invests a single dollar in anything, they need a financial buffer that lets them weather emergencies without cashing out their long-term plans or going into debt.

Emergency Fund Rule of Thumb
3–6 months of essential expenses in liquid, accessible savings

Single / Renter: 3 months · Married / Property owner: 6 months
Self-employed / Business owner: 9–12 months

The Savings Hierarchy

Help clients build savings in this order — many try to invest before they have a foundation, which forces them to liquidate at the worst time:

1

Emergency Fund (Tier 1)

High-yield savings account, money market fund, or short-term endowment. Accessible within 1–5 business days. This is not for investing — it is for sleeping well at night.

2

Specific Goals (Tier 2)

Known future expenses: property downpayment, renovation, wedding, car. Time-boxed. Endowments or short-duration products work well here.

3

Long-Term Savings (Tier 3)

Retirement, children's university, legacy. Longer time horizon — can afford more volatility. This is where investment products come in.

The 50/30/20 Framework

A simple budgeting lens to use with clients who feel like they "have no money to save":

50% — Needs

Housing, food, transport, utilities, insurance premiums. Non-negotiable.

30% — Wants

Dining out, entertainment, holidays, shopping. Lifestyle spending.

20% — Future

Savings, investments, insurance premiums, CPF top-ups. Pay yourself first.

💡FC Tip: When a client says "I can only afford $200/month," help them see the 50/30/20 breakdown of their income. Often they are spending 40%+ on wants without realising it. This conversation is not judgemental — it is illuminating.
Pillar 3

Investment & Wealth Building

Once protection is in place and an emergency fund exists, surplus capital should work harder than a savings account. Investment planning is about matching the client's goals, timeline, and risk appetite to the right vehicles.

The Risk-Return Spectrum

Risk vs Return — Key Asset Classes

Investment Principles to Teach Clients

⏳ Time in Market vs Timing the Market

The investor who stays invested through downturns almost always outperforms the one who tries to time entry and exit. A client who invests $1,000/month for 30 years at 7% grows to $1.2M — waiting 5 years to "find the right time" costs them $270K.

🔄 Dollar-Cost Averaging

Regular monthly contributions automatically buy more units when markets are low and fewer when high. Over time this smooths out volatility. This is exactly how ILP regular premium works — built-in DCA.

🌍 Diversification

Don't concentrate in one company, sector, or geography. Spread across asset classes and markets. In Singapore, many clients are over-concentrated in property — this is a risk conversation you should have.

🧠 Behavioural Finance

Clients make emotional decisions with money — panic-selling in downturns, chasing performance. Your role is to be the rational voice that keeps them on track. "How would you feel if this portfolio dropped 20% temporarily?"

ILP Explained Simply

Investment-Linked Plans are one of the most commonly misunderstood products. Here is a client-friendly way to explain them:

The ILP Explanation (Non-jargon Version)

"An ILP is essentially two things in one — a life insurance policy and an investment account. Your premium buys units in investment funds of your choice. Part of that premium pays for your life insurance coverage, and the rest stays invested and grows over time. Unlike a savings plan, the value can go up or down based on how the funds perform. But over the long term — 10, 20, 30 years — it is designed to grow your wealth while keeping you covered."

Retirement Planning

The CPF Foundation

CPF is the backbone of Singapore's retirement system. Every FC needs to understand the basics:

The Retirement Gap

Retirement Needs Calculation
Monthly Expenses in Retirement × 12 × 25 years = Total Fund Needed

Minus: Projected CPF Life payout × 12 × 25 years = Private Savings Gap

Example: $4,000/month × 300 months = $1.2M needed
CPF Life (Full RS): ~$1,700/month × 300 = $510,000
Private gap to fill: ~$690,000

SRS — The Tax Advantage

Supplementary Retirement Scheme contributions are fully tax-deductible up to $15,300/year for Singapore Citizens/PRs ($35,700 for foreigners). Withdrawals after age 62 are taxed at 50% of the amount — so the effective tax rate is very low. For clients in the 11.5%+ bracket, this is a no-brainer conversation.

💡Every client earning above $60,000/year should know about SRS. It is one of the most underutilised tax tools in Singapore. If your client doesn't have an SRS account, open that conversation immediately.

University education in Singapore — and especially overseas — is one of the largest predictable expenses a family faces. The key word is predictable: unlike medical emergencies, you know this is coming. Which means there is no excuse not to plan for it.

The Education Planning Conversation

When you have a client with a young child (especially 0–7 years), education planning is one of the easiest conversations to open:

Opening Script

"Congratulations on your little one! Have you thought about where you'd like them to study? Local universities are around $40,000 over four years today — but with inflation, that could be $60,000–70,000 by the time they're ready. If you'd prefer overseas, we're looking at $150,000–300,000. The good news is — with 15+ years to save, even a small monthly commitment now can grow to cover most of that. Shall I show you the numbers?"

The Power of Starting Early

Education Fund Target: $150,000 (overseas degree) in 18 years
Monthly savings needed at 5% return from age 0: ~$420/month
Monthly savings needed at 5% return from age 8: ~$870/month
Monthly savings needed at 5% return from age 13: ~$1,850/month

This single comparison — shown to a parent — often closes the education endowment conversation immediately. The earlier they start, the less it costs. Waiting is literally the most expensive decision they can make.

Pillar 6

Estate Planning

Estate planning is not about death — it is about control. Who gets what. Who decides. Who protects the family when you can't. Many clients avoid this topic because it feels morbid. Your job is to reframe it.

The Reframe That Opens the Conversation

"Estate planning isn't about preparing to die. It's about making sure the people you love are taken care of exactly the way you'd want — whether you're here or not, whether you're well or not. It's one of the most loving things you can do for your family."

The 5 Estate Planning Tools

📋 Will (Wasiat)

Legally binding document directing how assets are distributed after death. Without one, the Intestate Succession Act applies — which may not match the client's wishes. Muslim clients: Wasiat is limited to 1/3 of estate to non-heirs.

🏦 Insurance Nominations

Nominees receive policy proceeds directly, bypassing probate. Trust nominations create a legally binding obligation. Critical to review when family situations change (marriage, divorce, children).

🏛️ Trust

Assets held by a trustee for beneficiaries. Useful for: protecting minors, controlling asset distribution timing, wealth preservation across generations, and protecting against potential future claimants.

📝 LPA (Lasting Power of Attorney)

Appoints a donee to make decisions on the client's behalf if they lose mental capacity. Covers property, financial matters, and personal welfare. Registered with the Office of the Public Guardian.

🏥 AMD (Advance Medical Directive)

Instructs doctors not to use extraordinary life-sustaining measures in the terminal stage of a terminal illness. A deeply personal document — but one many clients have never heard of.

🇸🇬 CPF Nomination

CPF monies do NOT pass through the will — a separate CPF nomination is needed. Without one, CPF goes to the Public Trustee's Office. For Muslim clients, CPF nominations are NOT governed by Faraid — they are civil law.

Client Journey

Gap Analysis

A gap analysis makes the invisible visible. It converts a vague sense of "I should probably have more insurance" into a specific, undeniable number that the client can act on.

Running the Gap Analysis

1

Map What They Have

List all existing policies, CPF, savings, investments. This is non-judgemental — just inventory. Use the SOUL FACTFinder to capture this systematically.

2

Calculate What They Need

Apply the HLV formula for life, use MAS benchmarks for CI (3–5× annual income), check hospitalisation plan class, estimate retirement fund requirement, quantify education goal.

3

Show the Gap

Present as a simple table: Need vs Have vs Gap. A clear number is more powerful than any sales script. Most clients have never seen this laid out before — and it is often sobering.

4

Prioritise the Gaps

Don't try to fill everything in one proposal. Rank gaps by urgency and impact. "Let's tackle your biggest risk first — your death coverage is most critical given your young family. We can work on the retirement gap in stages."

The SOUL FACTFinder Excel tool calculates HLV, protection gaps, retirement shortfall, and education funding needs in real-time as you enter client data. Use it in every first meeting. It does the analysis — your job is to ask the right questions and explain the numbers.

Client Journey

Presenting the Financial Plan

The presentation meeting is where your fact-finding and analysis pays off. Done well, it doesn't feel like a sales pitch — it feels like a doctor explaining test results and recommending treatment. The client already knows the problem. You're presenting the solution.

The Presentation Structure

1

Recap What You Heard (3 min)

Start by playing back the client's own priorities and concerns. "When we met last week, you shared that your biggest concern was X, and that you wanted to make sure Y." This proves you listened and builds trust before you say anything else.

2

Present the Gap (5 min)

Show the before picture — where they are now, what gaps exist, what risks they are currently carrying. Let the numbers speak. Don't editorialize. "Based on your current situation, if something were to happen to you today, your family would face a shortfall of approximately $X."

3

Introduce the Solution (10 min)

Present one primary recommendation aligned with their stated priority. Then 1–2 secondary recommendations. Keep it focused. Too many options = paralysis. Explain each in plain language. No jargon without definition.

4

Handle Questions / Objections (10 min)

Invite questions. "What questions do you have?" not "Do you have any questions?" The former invites engagement; the latter signals you expect none. Common objections and how to handle them are in the SPIN Selling module.

5

Close or Schedule Next Step (5 min)

If they're ready: complete the application. If not: agree on a specific next step — "Let's meet again Thursday — I'll bring the final numbers and we can proceed." Never leave without a defined next action.

The Annual Review

The clients who stay with you for life are the ones you review annually — not just sell to once. The annual review is where you demonstrate ongoing value, deepen the relationship, and naturally uncover new needs.

Annual Review Agenda

The 5-Point Review Checklist

  • Life changes — marriage, children, divorce, promotion, property, health events in the past 12 months
  • Coverage review — has their income grown? Are their sum assured levels still appropriate?
  • Portfolio performance — for investment clients: fund performance, rebalancing if needed
  • CPF update — nomination current? SA shielding done? MediSave Basic Healthcare Sum met?
  • Estate documents — will updated? LPA in place? Insurance nominations current? Any new estate concerns?
💡Frame the annual review as a service standard, not a sales call. "This is something I do for all my clients every year — it's part of how we make sure your plan keeps up with your life." Clients who receive proactive reviews refer significantly more than those who don't.

Singapore Context

MAS & FAA Essentials

As a financial consultant licensed under the Financial Advisers Act, you have legal and ethical obligations to every client. These are not optional extras — they are the foundation of professional practice.

Your Core Obligations

📋 Needs-Based Selling

You must conduct a Financial Needs Analysis (FNA) before making any recommendation. The product must be suitable for the client's circumstances, needs, and financial situation. MAS can and does investigate complaints about unsuitable advice.

💡 Disclosure Requirements

You must disclose: your commission structure, any conflicts of interest, product fees and charges, and key risks. Clients must receive a Product Summary and cover page. No shortcuts on documentation.

📁 Record Keeping

All FNA, client documents, and recommendations must be kept for at least 5 years. Digital or physical. If a client complains and you have no records, the presumption is against you.

🔄 Ongoing Duty of Care

Your duty doesn't end at the point of sale. If you become aware of a material change in a client's circumstances, you have an obligation to reassess the suitability of their existing coverage.

⚠️Key Risk: The most common MAS complaint area is churning — replacing existing policies unnecessarily to generate commission. Analyse objectively. Document your reasoning. Every replacement must be justified by clear client benefit, not by your commission calendar.

Common Client Scenarios

These are the client types you will encounter most frequently as a SOUL Guild FC. Recognising the scenario quickly helps you go from small talk to value-adding conversation faster.

👨‍👩‍👧 Scenario 1: Young Married Couple, First Child on the Way

Key concerns: What happens to the baby if we both died? Can we afford education? Can we keep up the mortgage?

Focus areas: Death/TPD (both earners), CI, mortgage protection, education endowment, will and nominations.

Common mistake FCs make: Selling only one spouse a policy. Both parents need coverage if both contribute to the household.

💼 Scenario 2: Self-Employed Professional, 38 Years Old

Key concerns: No employer benefits. No group insurance. What if I can't work?

Focus areas: Disability income (most urgent — no sick leave), CI, hospitalisation, larger emergency fund (9–12 months), SRS (tax advantage).

Common mistake: Underestimating disability risk. A 38-year-old has a 25% chance of being disabled for 90+ days before retirement. Most are zero-covered.

🏠 Scenario 3: HDB Upgrader, 45 Years Old

Key concerns: Just took on a large private property loan. Retirement feels far away but isn't.

Focus areas: Mortgage protection top-up, retirement gap (CPF heavily used for property), CI review, LPA, will review.

Key insight: Upgrading the property often means upgrading the risk. The HLV gap grows even as the client feels more financially "successful."

🧓 Scenario 4: Pre-Retiree, 58 Years Old

Key concerns: Am I ready? Do I have enough? What about healthcare costs?

Focus areas: CPF optimisation (RA top-up strategy), SRS withdrawals, PRUShield review, annuity options, estate distribution planning, LPA if not yet done.

Opportunity: Many 58-year-olds have accumulated assets but no organised plan. A good FC who does a proper retirement projection becomes their trusted advisor for the next 20+ years.

Final Check — Putting It Together

A 42-year-old Muslim client tells you: "I recently became a single parent after my divorce. My two kids are 8 and 11. I own a HDB flat with a remaining loan of $200,000. My income is $7,000/month and I currently have no insurance." Which financial planning area is MOST urgent?
Education planning — the children will need university funding in 10 years.
Retirement — at 42, there's still time but it should be addressed now.
Protection — a single parent with zero insurance, two dependent children, and a $200K mortgage is carrying catastrophic risk. If something happens to them tomorrow, the children have no financial safety net.
✓ Correct. Protection is the absolute priority. The client is the sole earner for two children with a mortgage — their human life value is enormous and they have zero coverage. Secondary: will + nominations + guardianship arrangements. Then circle back to education and retirement once the foundation is in place. Also note: as a Muslim client, Faraid and halal estate planning tools should be incorporated into the estate planning conversation.
✗ While these are valid considerations, protection is the most urgent. The client has no financial safety net for their children. A $7,000/month earner with zero insurance, two dependants, and a mortgage faces catastrophic risk if anything happens to them.
